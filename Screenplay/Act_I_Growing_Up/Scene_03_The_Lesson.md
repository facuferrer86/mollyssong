**INT. UNDERGROUND LAB - DAY**

A corner of the lab has been transformed into a makeshift classroom. A piece of salvaged metal serves as a blackboard, propped against the wall. Chalk marks and erasure smudges show this is a well-used space.

MOLLY (12) sits cross-legged on a cushion, a worn history textbook open in her lap. Her notebook is filled with careful handwriting.

ELIAS stands before the makeshift blackboard, chalk in hand. He writes "MAGNA CARTA - 1215" at the top, then draws three columns below it.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> Today we're examining three concepts that changed the course of human history.

He writes "JUSTICE" in the first column.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> First, justice. The idea that laws should protect everyone equally, not just the powerful.

He writes "ACCOUNTABILITY" in the second column.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> Second, accountability. The idea that even kings must answer to the law.

He writes "LEGITIMACY" in the third column.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> And third, legitimacy. That rightful power must be earned and accepted, not simply taken by force.

He turns to face Molly, tapping the board with his chalk.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> You've read the chapter. Before the Magna Carta, what did justice look like for common people?

<center>MOLLY</center>
> There wasn't any. The king could tax anyone, imprison anyone, take anyone's property. There were no protections.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> And what did the Magna Carta create?

<center>MOLLY</center>
> Rules. Limits. The promise that justice would be the same for everyone, not just what the king wanted.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> Good. But why is accountability so important? What does that tell us about power?

<center>MOLLY</center>
> That power without limits becomes dangerous. Even if the person starts out good.

Elias writes on the board: "POWER + NO LIMITS = CORRUPTION"

<center>ELIAS</center>
> Now, imagine you had unlimited power. You could fix every problem, end every conflict, make everyone safe. Would you use it?

<center>MOLLY</center>
> (Without hesitation)
> Yes.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> (A sad smile)
> And that's exactly why you'd be dangerous.

Molly looks puzzled. Elias sits down across from her.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> The road to tyranny is paved with good intentions, Molly. The most dangerous rulers are often the ones who truly believe they know what's best for everyone.

<center>MOLLY</center>
> But what if you really do know what's best?

<center>ELIAS</center>
> (Quietly)
> That's the question that keeps me awake at night.

There's a weight to his words that Molly doesn't fully understand. But she sees the pain in his eyes.

<center>MOLLY</center>
> The Magna Carta made the king accountable to law, right? But where did the law get its legitimacy?

<center>ELIAS</center>
> (Proud)
> Excellent question. That took another few centuries to figure out.

He stands and writes under "LEGITIMACY": "AUTHORITY FROM THE PEOPLE"

<center>ELIAS</center>
> What happens when decisions are made by many voices instead of one?

<center>MOLLY</center>
> It takes longer. But... more perspectives get heard?

<center>ELIAS</center>
> And what does that require from the decision-makers?

<center>MOLLY</center>
> (Understanding dawning)
> Empathy. You have to be able to understand other people's experiences.

Elias nods, his expression serious.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> Without empathy, justice dies. People stop listening to each other. They stop seeing each other as human.

The lesson hangs in the air. Molly looks down at her textbook, then back at her father.

<center>MOLLY</center>
> Is that what happened to the world outside? People stopped listening?

Elias is quiet for a long moment. Then he stands and begins erasing the board.

<center>ELIAS</center>
> Tomorrow we'll discuss the Bill of Rights. Read Chapter 8 tonight.

He doesn't answer her question directly, but his silence says everything.
